The principles of reforestation defended by the association

Extracts from an article written by Michel Thinon, research scientist at the C.N.R.S.
(National Center for Scientific Research)   

* The methods of reforestation used since 1987 by the A.R.P.C.V. after severe fire ravaged the Montagne Sainte Victoire, differ from those usually employed which take as a basic principle that the Mediterranean forest is, by nature, made up of holly oak and pines.  Hence, despite its success, the A.R.P.C.V. has been open to question, criticism, and at times, frank hostility.

* The Association’s conception of forestation reposes on scientific observation which includes the dimensions of history.  The varying disciplines dealing with the study of plants provide undeniable proof that the Mediterranean vegetation as we know it nowadays is not natural.  It is nothing but the way Nature has found to adapt itself to the constant aggressions of Man over the past 6000 years.

* In fact, originally, Mediterranean vegetation was dominated by  deciduous oaks like the downy oak.  From 5000 BC onwards, Man adopted a new lifestyle in that he started to produce his own food -  this was the “Neolithic revolution”.   Fire was constantly  used for the clearing and upkeep of pastureland and cultivated areas.  Around 2000 BC, at a period of stable climate, forests had practically disappeared  - the scrubland familiar to us all had become the norm.

* The Mediterranean landscape as we know it nowadays appears therefore to be the result of a man-made syndrome : fire – pastureland.  The vegetation that best stands up to such treatment was gradually selected over thousands of years and it now makes up for the major part of what is called natural land and its particular sensitivity to fire can be traced right back to this.

* In order to put an end to this logic of planting what will fuel tomorrow’s fires, the (re)creation of less sensitive woodland should be envisaged and were there fresh outbreaks of fire , investments in planting would then be unnecessary.  Only rational forestation which integrates ecological imperatives will achieve this double objective.
